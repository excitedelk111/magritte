Magritte VR

Overview

This VR project was part of an Experimental Collaborative Scholarly Course at Brown called "Simulating Reality". The original idea was to just create a 3D rendition of Magritte’s Painting, La Vengeance, in VR. There is nothing more challenging than recreating the works of Surrealist artists in 3D, especially the work of someone like Magritte, as surrealism is meant to depict illogical, unrealistic scenes and his work is some of the best, if not the best. After a bit of work on the project we decided to expand its scope to take full advantage of what VR offers. Magritte questions the status of representation in his paintings. We felt like the best way to translate that intention into the modern world would be to have a VR experience that allows users to experience both possible realities in this painting.

The Experience

The viewer will be able to walk around the room and enter the painting within the painting, a second ‘reality’. The user is able to switch between a landscape painting and clouds painted onto the wall to a window with real mountains and real clouds.

Texturing and Shading

We created these paint textures in Photoshop and Blender to mimic the texture of the original painting. Some of the textures were done using Image Projection (such as the metal ball).
One of our biggest challenges was creating the shader for the 3D scene inside the painting, so that only the parts contained inside the painting would be seen, and the rest hidden.
We found an existing resource online on something called a Stencil Buffer, adapted it by writing our own shaders that preserved the texture of the objects but also allowed them to “disappear” outside certain boundaries. We figured out variations of the script we found online for the easel, clouds, mountains, painting frame, which were all using slightly different combinations of code.

Equipment

We initially only had access to an HTC Vive, but after setting up our project, we realised that there were many limitations. The roof in the model was low and so the painting appeared small and this was because the physical room is small and the HTC Vive's recognizable free space was bounded by the physical room in which it was set up. We were able to touch two physical walls but not the other two. We could have possibly set dressed the space so people could feel more with their hands while exploring the space - prop walls, easel and canvas? but does that take away or add something? We eventually got access to an Oculus Quest 2 and used it for our project which addressed all the limitations that were associated with the Vive. After setting it all up, we also realised that it was very difficult to get the users’ eye-line to line up with the exact point we would need them to be at for a seamless transition between the 3D clouds (real) and projected clouds (painting), so, ee decided to use a button that allows users to click to switch between the 2 forms. Alternatively, we could teleport people back to the exact point and remove the walking component, but we felt like what made this project more immersive was the ability to walk around the room.
